[continuing the previous poll]
I gave the idea some more thought, and I'm now leaning towards the second option in the poll instead.

I think it would help to group force automata and cellular automata in a general "automata" class, where the distinguishing features between each automaton is what exactly its "rules" are.

In a cellular automaton, each cell is completely independent from its neighbors and only updates its own state each tick based on the state of the neighborhood around it. The "rule" is how each cell updates itself. Because of this property, the order of which cells are updates does not matter as it simply depends on the initial state of the tick. The locality is that neighborhoods are fixed and non-arbitrary.

In a force automaton, each cell is not necessarily independent from its neighbors and can directly influence the state of itself and other cells by propagating forces; the state of its neighborhood is not necessarily relevant to how it updates. The "rule" is how each cell chooses to exert forces and how each force works. Since cells aren't independent of each other, the order of the automaton does matter, and can lead to various types of "biases". The locality is that forces are not able to affect any arbitrary cell, and are instead relative to the cell which exerted it to begin with.

This much more lines up with the assumed idea. Cellular automata and force automata are both automata, but the behavior of cells and how rules work separate the two.

Separate from the topic of equivalency, I can see a way to model a cellular automaton in terms of forces, but I don't know if there's a way to model a force automaton in terms of neighborhoods (at least with a bounded amount of them).

​@wetboatguy's ‪video refuting his model as being a cellular automaton brought to my attention that the view of Cell Machine and its descendants as cellular automata is not as clear-cut as I thought it was. I find this interesting, but I think the reasoning for it isn't correct—or at least too narrow.
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fycWBS6Nkrs

[IMAGE: A Mover moves forward into a row of Pushables with an Enemy at the end. The Mover pushes the row, destroying the Enemy on the same tick.]
In the video, one of the points used to analyze it as not a CA is that "information" is able to be passed instantaneously across several cells—that is, the typical "speed of light" (defined by the speed of a normal moving contraption) is broken. Such a feat is impossible in Conway's Game of Life and more importantly violates locality. In the example above, the Mover is able to destroy the Enemy across an arbitrary distance instantly.

[IMAGE: The Mover exerts a pushing force onto itself, which propagates forward cell by cell until it hits the enemy. Once the force can no longer propagate, all the cells move forward.]
I personally believe that the simulation is better reframed in terms of "forces" rather than "rules". The "rule" is that the Moving cell moves one space forward every tick, but the underlying "force" is that it pushes itself, and that checks the cell in front to determine if the force propagates or not. This is still local, as the force is only dependent on the current cell and its immediate neighbor rather than the entire structure, even if on the surface it looks global. This gets a little muddy in CelLua Machine's case with cells like the Adjustable Slope and Portal, but works as a perfect interpretation of Cell Machine and wetboatguy's CA. I like to call these kinds of CA's "force automata". I could go as far as to say that traditional CAs are subsets of FAs, but that's much more speculative.

Of course, I don't know the full picture. I don't actually study automata theory and I'm personally biased. So, I'll run a poll to see everyone's opinions. Are Cell Machine and its descendants cellular automata?

in the traditional pedalism system used by everyone on earth, these would appropriately be named "monopedal" and "no legged"

[On the left is the same cat with crutches, actively walking. The two arms holding the crutches are labeled "both moving together" and "both stabilize independently", while the one leg is said to "move and stabilize independently". It is overall "bimotile tristable".
On the right is the same limbless cat. The base of its body is highlighted, and it is said to be "antimotile monostable upright". Below, it is seen moving across the floor like a worm, and is labeled "monoped lying down".]

however, under my pedality system, what determines the pedality of a creature is based upon the parts of it which can drive motion/force (the motors) and retain the current context (the anchors)

the left example would be bimotile tristable. the arms (not the crutches) work together and in unison to complete a single force, so they are a single motor, however cannot stabilize alone, making them two distinct anchors. the leg is its own motor and anchor. together, this gives them two motors and three anchors. a good comparison would be assuming the crutches are actually raised platforms. for the creature to truly be a monoped (monomotile monostable), they would need to rely on their leg alone without crutches

the right example would be considered antimotile monostable, as there are no motors, but there is an anchor (the base of the body). however, this is only in the context of them being upright. lying on the floor, they can squirm around and such and move their body that way, allowing them to be completely monopedal as the body acts as both a motor and an anchor
